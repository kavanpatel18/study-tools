01

Spaced Repetition

Review at growing intervals to lock memories in long-term storage
Easy to start

The "forgetting curve" (Ebbinghaus, 1885) shows we lose ~50% of new info within an hour. Reviewing just before you forget — and spacing reviews further apart each time — exploits how the brain consolidates memory during sleep and rest. Studies show spaced repetition can reduce study time by up to 50% vs. massed practice.

Step-by-Step Practice
✓
Step 1 — Identify
Write down 10–20 facts, definitions, or concepts you need to learn for your exam. These become your "cards."
💡 Keep each item atomic — one idea per card.
✓
Step 2 — First Review
Study all your cards today. Rate each 1–3 after reviewing: 1 = forgot, 2 = struggled, 3 = knew it.
✓
Step 3 — Schedule Next Reviews
Use this interval ladder: Day 1 → Day 3 → Day 7 → Day 14 → Day 30. Hard cards (rated 1) go back to Day 1. Easy cards (rated 3) jump ahead.
💡 Apps like Anki do this automatically — highly recommended.
✓
Step 4 — Daily Review Sessions
Spend 15–20 min/day reviewing only due cards. Don't cram — consistency over time beats marathon sessions.
✓
Step 5 — The Night Before
Do one final pass of all cards. Sleep 7–8 hours. Memory consolidation happens during sleep — this is not optional!
💡 Your brain replays memories during REM sleep. Sleeping after review is as important as the review itself.

Learning the Krebs cycle steps for a biology exam? Create one card per step (8 total). Review all 8 today. Tomorrow review any you missed. In 3 days review all again. By exam week, you'll only need to review 2–3 shaky ones.

02

Active Recall

Force your brain to retrieve — not re-read
Easy to start

The "testing effect" (Roediger & Karpicke, 2006) shows that retrieving information from memory strengthens it far more than passively re-reading. Students who practiced retrieval scored 50% higher on delayed tests than re-readers. The harder the retrieval, the stronger the memory.

Step-by-Step Practice
✓
Step 1 — Read Once, Then Close
Read a section of your notes or textbook ONE time. Then close it. Do not re-read. This feels uncomfortable — that's the point.
✓
Step 2 — Brain Dump
On a blank sheet of paper, write everything you can remember from what you just read. Don't peek. Incomplete is fine — you're training retrieval, not copying.
💡 Use a timer — 5 minutes of recall beats 20 minutes of re-reading.
✓
Step 3 — Check & Mark Gaps
Open your notes and compare. Circle or highlight everything you missed. These gaps are your study priorities — not what you already know.
✓
Step 4 — Question Everything
Turn each key fact into a question. "The mitochondria is the powerhouse of the cell" → "What is the function of the mitochondria?" Quiz yourself with questions, not answers.
✓
Step 5 — Practice Past Papers
Use old exam questions as active recall practice. Do them under timed, closed-book conditions. This is the single highest-impact study activity for exams.
💡 Even 10-minute "mini exams" from memory are powerful.

After reading a chapter on the French Revolution, close the book and write down every cause, key figure, and date you can recall. You remember 6 of 12 causes? Perfect — now study only the 6 you missed.

03

Method of Loci

Anchor information to vivid locations in a mental journey
Medium effort

Used by ancient Greek orators, this technique exploits the brain's exceptional spatial memory. Neuroimaging shows that memory champions use the hippocampus (spatial navigation region) rather than rote memorization pathways. A 2017 study trained beginners in 40 days — they went from average to top 20% in memory competitions.

Step-by-Step Practice
✓
Step 1 — Choose Your Palace
Pick a place you know intimately: your home, your commute route, your school. This is your "memory palace." You'll mentally walk through it the same way every time.
✓
Step 2 — Define Your Stations
Identify 10–15 distinct "stations" along your route (front door → hallway → kitchen counter → fridge → etc.). These become fixed memory slots.
💡 Unusual or slightly absurd locations work better — they're more memorable.
✓
Step 3 — Create Vivid Images
For each fact, create an exaggerated, bizarre, or funny image. The more emotionally vivid and weird, the better. Movement, smell, sound — engage all senses.
✓
Step 4 — Place Images at Stations
Mentally "walk" your palace and place each image at its station. Spend 5 seconds vividly imagining the scene — don't rush this step.
✓
Step 5 — Walk It to Retrieve
To recall, mentally walk the palace from start to finish. Each station should trigger its image, which triggers the fact. Practice the walk 3× over 2 days.
💡 You can reuse the palace for new content — just "clear" the old images first.

Memorizing the order of planets: At your front door, a giant Mercury thermometer explodes. In the hallway, a beautiful Venus flytrap eats your shoes. In the kitchen, a blue marble (Earth) rolls across the counter. A red tomato (Mars) splats on the fridge... and so on.

04

Chunking

Group information into meaningful clusters to expand memory capacity
Easy to start

Working memory holds only ~4 items at once (Miller's revised "magical number"). Chunking beats this limit by grouping items into meaningful units — each chunk counts as ONE item. Chess grandmasters don't memorize 32 pieces; they memorize ~5 "patterns." Expertise is largely the accumulation of powerful chunks.

Step-by-Step Practice
✓
Step 1 — List Everything First
Write out all the items you need to memorize without organizing them. For a 30-item list, get everything on paper before grouping.
✓
Step 2 — Find Natural Groups
Look for categories, patterns, causes/effects, timelines, or themes. Group your items into clusters of 3–5. Aim for 5–7 chunks total from a long list.
✓
Step 3 — Name Each Chunk
Give each group a memorable label — ideally one word or a short phrase. This "chunk header" becomes the hook that pulls the whole group from memory.
💡 The label should feel meaningful to you, not generic. "Disaster causes" beats "Group A."
✓
Step 4 — Learn Chunk Labels First
Memorize just the chunk names until you can recall them all from memory. Then layer in the items within each chunk. Two-level recall is far more reliable.
✓
Step 5 — Draw a Mind Map
Visually map your chunks with the topic at center, chunk labels as branches, and items as leaves. Hand-drawing activates deeper encoding than typing.

Learning WW2 causes? Instead of 12 random facts, chunk into: "MAIN" causes (Militarism, Alliances, Imperialism, Nationalism) + Treaty failures + Economic collapse. Now you only need to recall 3 chunks — and the details follow naturally.

05

Mnemonics

Encode facts into rhymes, acronyms, and stories your brain won't forget
Easy to start

Mnemonics work by attaching new, arbitrary information to something already in long-term memory (words, rhythm, images). They exploit dual-coding (verbal + visual) and the brain's preference for narrative and pattern. Mnemonic learners outperform rote memorizers on recall tests by 40–60% in multiple studies.

Step-by-Step Practice
✓
Step 1 — Identify What Needs Anchoring
Mnemonics shine for: ordered lists, hard-to-spell terms, arbitrary facts (dates, names, formulas), and abstract concepts. Identify your 5 hardest-to-remember items.
✓
Step 2 — Choose Your Mnemonic Type
Acronym: ROYGBIV for rainbow colors. Acrostic: "My Very Energetic Mother Just Served Us Nachos" for planets. Rhyme: "In 1492, Columbus sailed the ocean blue." Story: Chain facts into a mini narrative.
✓
Step 3 — Make It Personal & Weird
The best mnemonics are ones you create yourself — involving your name, your friends, or absurd situations. Personalization dramatically improves encoding.
💡 A bad mnemonic you invented beats a perfect one someone else made.
✓
Step 4 — Test the Mnemonic
Write the mnemonic. Cover it. Can you reconstruct the original list from the mnemonic alone? If not, revise it until you can reliably decode it.
✓
Step 5 — Use Spaced Repetition on Mnemonics
Mnemonics aren't permanent — combine them with spaced repetition. Review the mnemonic → recall the original items → space out reviews over days.

Remembering the cranial nerves? "Oh Oh Oh To Touch And Feel Very Good Velvet, AH!" — where each first letter matches: Olfactory, Optic, Oculomotor, Trochlear, Trigeminal, Abducens, Facial, Vestibulocochlear, Glossopharyngeal, Vagus, Accessory, Hypoglossal.

06

The Feynman Technique

If you can't explain it simply, you don't understand it yet
Deep work

Named after Nobel Prize-winning physicist Richard Feynman, this technique targets understanding — not surface memorization. Cognitive science calls this "elaborative interrogation." Explaining forces your brain to identify exactly where knowledge breaks down, producing targeted, durable learning. Studies show it produces 2× better transfer to new problems vs. re-reading.

Step-by-Step Practice
✓
Step 1 — Choose a Concept
Pick one concept — not a whole chapter. "Natural selection," "supply and demand," "the chain rule." Single concepts only. Write it at the top of a blank page.
✓
Step 2 — Explain It to a Child
Write out an explanation as if teaching a 10-year-old. Use simple words. No jargon. No acronyms. If you can't simplify it, that reveals what you don't truly understand.
💡 Speak it aloud — explaining verbally exposes gaps faster than writing.
✓
Step 3 — Find the Gaps
Where did you get stuck? Where did you use jargon because you couldn't simplify? Where did your explanation feel fuzzy or circular? Circle these. They are your real study targets.
✓
Step 4 — Go Back to Source Material
Study only the gaps you found. Read the textbook or notes specifically for the points you couldn't explain. Then close everything and write the explanation again from scratch.
✓
Step 5 — Simplify with Analogies
Create an analogy that maps the concept to something familiar. A good analogy means you understand deeply. "The immune system is like a bouncer with a guest list" beats "the immune system provides adaptive defense."
💡 Exam essays that use accurate analogies consistently score higher.

Trying to explain entropy: "Things naturally get more disordered over time — like your room after a week without tidying." Stuck on why? Go back, read about thermodynamics, then explain again. The second explanation will be far cleaner — and you'll remember it for years.
